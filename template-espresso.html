Coffee Recipe of the Week

The Café Cubano Cloud

Café Cubano Cloud

Inspired by Little Havana, Miami

The original café cubano is one of the most culturally specific coffees in the world — a double ristretto pulled directly onto demerara sugar, which the crema emulsifies into a pale, silky espuma on top. This version adds one cold element: a single ice cube in a warm demitasse glass that chills the bottom while the hot crema floats above. Two temperatures. One cup. The kind of coffee that has an opinion about itself.

  1. 1.Place one teaspoon of demerara sugar in a small metal or glass espresso cup. The sugar must be dark — white sugar won't create the same espuma.
  2. 2.Pull a double ristretto (18g in, 25–30ml out, shorter and sweeter than espresso). Pull it directly onto the sugar.
  3. 3.Immediately stir with energy — thirty seconds minimum. You are creating the espuma: the sugar and crema should emulsify into a pale, almost caramel-coloured foam.
  4. 4.Drop a single small ice cube into a wider glass. Pour the espuma-topped coffee over it in one slow motion.
  5. 5.Do not stir again. Drink it in three or four sips. This coffee is not for lingering.
Coffee Shop of the Week

Panther Coffee

Wynwood, Miami

Founded in 2010 by Joel and Leticia Pollock, Panther has been roasting seriously in Miami since before Wynwood became the kind of place people go to be photographed. The Wynwood location has a garden — unusual for a building surrounded by murals — and a long communal table where the conversations that matter in Miami happen. They source single-origin with the rigour of a European specialty roaster and serve it with the warmth of somewhere that has never been trying to impress you. The Honduran on filter right now is exceptional. Go before the race weekend makes the neighbourhood impossible.

Woman in Motorsport

Who inspires us this week

Jamie Chadwick

Racing Driver — Andretti IndyCar, Former W Series Champion

In 2019, Jamie Chadwick became the first woman to win the British Formula Ford Festival, one of the oldest and most competitive junior single-seater races in the world. She was twenty-one. She then won the W Series — the all-female single-seater championship — three consecutive times, in 2019, 2021, and 2022. She became a development driver for Williams F1. She tested, she participated in practice sessions, she was consistently faster than the data suggested she should have been. The door to a race seat did not open.

In 2024, Chadwick joined Andretti Global in IndyCar — one of the most competitive open-wheel series in the world. It was a significant step. IndyCar is not Formula 1, but it is not a lesser thing: it is a different kind of challenge, a series that rewards versatility and racecraft and the ability to adapt to ovals, street circuits, and road courses within the same season. She has been competitive, not yet winning, still learning. The fact that she made it here at all — that she kept going after the F1 door stayed closed — is the more interesting story. The most common version of this story ends in resignation. Hers hasn't.

What we find most worth thinking about with Jamie Chadwick is not the records she has set — though those matter — but the specific quality of persistence that her career represents. She has operated for years in a space where the structural barriers were real, where the question of whether a woman could compete at the highest level was still being argued as if it were a legitimate open question. She answered it, repeatedly, with lap times. And then she kept going.

Pinterest Picks

Three accounts for this Sunday
@wynwoodwalls

Wynwood Walls Official

The official account of the Miami street art district. What it documents — murals, artists, the conversation between walls — is the visual grammar that this city runs on. Follow it before race week as preparation for understanding what you're actually looking at when you're there.

@racingarchivesfilm

Racing Archives Film

Analogue photography of Formula 1 from the 1960s to the 1990s. Every post is a reminder that the most beautiful era of the sport was also the most dangerous, and that beauty and danger have always been exactly that entangled. The colour work from the 1970s is particularly extraordinary.

@paddock.edit

Paddock Edit

The most thoughtful curation of what is actually being worn in the paddock — focused on the people who have something to say beyond the obvious brand placements. Heavily female-weighted in its focus. The kind of account that makes you realise the paddock fashion conversation is more interesting than it gets credit for.
